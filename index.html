Cellular Automata Blog

Juan Castillo · April 21, 2026

Design Goal

My first reaction to the completed lab was that I was very happy, because it was really satisfying to click play and hear random sounds come out of a system I had built myself. I wanted the project to feel playful instead of purely technical, so I used a randomized 12 by 12 grid that evolves with Conway’s Game of Life rules and treated it like a small instrument rather than just a coding exercise.

Finding the Mapping

The biggest trial-and-error process was figuring out how to map the automaton to sound, because I tried rows, columns, combinations, and cluster-based ideas before settling on something that actually felt musical. That was also the moment the lab opened up for me, because I realized I could be creative and change the rules around what sounded best instead of assuming there was one correct mapping.

Why the Largest Cluster

The largest-cluster version sounded the nicest to me because it was peaceful and satisfying without becoming overwhelming. In code, I run a connected-cluster search each generation, treat diagonal cells as connected, and use the largest cluster as the musical source: its vertical centroid maps to pitch in a minor pentatonic scale, and its size affects how strong the note feels. That made the sound respond to structure, not just to the number of active cells.

Sound Design

Another big lesson was that sound design mattered just as much as the mapping. Early versions sounded bad because there was almost no attack or release shaping, so the notes felt abrupt and flat; I fixed that mostly by listening and adjusting the envelope until the sound started to breathe better. The final Web Audio voice is a softer pad made from layered triangle oscillators, a sine sub, and a low-pass filter sweep, which fit the arcade-inspired Tetris look while still sounding calm.

What I Learned by Building It

The biggest thing I learned is that I can be my own composer even if the instrument is just simple code. Before this, automated composition felt more distant and technical to me, but building it myself showed me that simple rules, small sound-design decisions, and careful listening can already make something expressive. The part I feel best about is that I genuinely enjoy the music and feel proud of what I built.

What Still Feels Open

One thing that still feels imperfect is that the piece does not go on forever, because eventually the automaton settles or dies out, but I also think that is part of what makes it cooler. Each run feels like a unique little performance, almost like jazz being shaped in front of your eyes, and if I kept working on it I would add more flowing animation tied to the cells since the visuals already make me pay closer attention to pitch, tone, and shape.

Takeaway

What stayed with me most from this lab is that automated composition is really an act of interpretation. The code handles the automaton updates, cluster detection, pitch mapping, and Web Audio scheduling, but the piece only starts to feel like music when those technical parts are shaped by taste and listening. That balance between system design and artistic choice is what made the whole project feel personal to me.
